What Makes Humor Land?

We've seen the data — but what about lived experience?

Stephen Turban, a Harvard-trained researcher turned stand-up comedian in Vietnam, shared what it's like to learn humor across cultures. Raised in the U.S., he now performs jokes in Vietnamese — a language and culture he didn't grow up with.

"The biggest barrier for me when learning comedy in Vietnamese wasn't the language — it was understanding what people believe."

To be funny, he explains, you need two things: something people haven't thought of — and something they believe to be true. Getting the first is easy as a foreigner. The second? Not so much.

"I might notice something weird, like how southerners sing karaoke in a northern accent. But unless everyone agrees it's true, the joke won't land."

He describes the challenge as "perspective-taking" — figuring out what people really believe, then twisting that truth just enough to surprise them.

Turban also highlights one classic technique:

"I love what I call a deliberate misdirection — like when a 19-year-old says she's dating someone with a five-year age difference, and you reply: 'Wow, what's it like dating a 14-year-old?'"

It's unexpected. It's borderline wrong. But it's clear enough to make people laugh.

Even in our data, we saw this pattern: funny captions often break expectations, flirt with absurdity, and twist something real just enough to surprise. In the end, good comedy requires both math and empathy — and sometimes, karaoke.

In short: Good captions are short, surprising, and strike a shared truth. Great ones do all three in ten words or less.
